Expert Assessment and Recommendations
1. Epoch Definition and Normalization Strategy
Your choice of using the full t=0–5s epoch with the t=0–2s pre-cue window as baseline is methodologically sound. A few important clarifications and caveats:

On the baseline window: At t=0s, the fixation cross already appears — this is a preparatory/alerting signal, not a fully neutral resting state. Ideally, a true neutral baseline would come from the inter-trial interval (before t=0s). However, since this is your recorded window and it is standard practice in the literature to use the pre-cue period this way, it is acceptable — just acknowledge this limitation when reporting.

On normalization: You mentioned using the baseline for normalization, but also using rest trials as a reference. These are two conceptually distinct operations that must not be conflated:

Within-trial baseline normalization (t=0–2s): normalizes the active window power relative to the preparatory period within each trial. This is the classical ERD/ERS framework.
Rest class as a reference condition: using rest trials as a comparison class in your between-class PSD analysis — i.e., "how does movement class X differ spectrally from rest?"
Both are valid and complementary. I recommend being explicit about which normalization is applied at which stage of the pipeline.

2. Welch vs. Multitaper — My Recommendation
With 5-second epochs at 512 Hz you have 2560 samples per epoch. Here is a concrete comparison:

Welch (50% overlap, 1-second segments):

Frequency resolution: 1 Hz
Approximately 9 overlapping segments per epoch
Very standard, widely used in motor EEG/BCI literature, easy to interpret and reproduce
Multitaper (time-bandwidth product TW=3, 5 tapers):

Frequency resolution: ~1.2 Hz
Better spectral concentration and variance reduction
Handles spectral leakage more rigorously
My recommendation: stay with Welch. Here is why:

For alpha (8–13 Hz, 5 Hz bandwidth) and beta (13–30 Hz, 17 Hz bandwidth), a 1 Hz resolution is more than sufficient to characterize band power and median frequency. Multitaper's advantage over Welch is most consequential when you need narrow-band spectral precision or when epochs are very short (<2s). With 5-second epochs and broad bands, the practical difference between the two methods is marginal. Welch is also the dominant method in the sensorimotor EEG literature, which makes your results directly comparable to prior work.

Specific Welch parameter recommendation: 1-second segments (512 samples), 50% overlap (256 samples), Hann window. This gives 1 Hz resolution with good variance reduction.

3. PSD Metrics — Assessment and Suggestions
Your two proposed metrics are well-chosen:

Area under the curve (band power): This is the integral of the PSD over the frequency band — exactly what the EEG community calls band power. It is the most widely used and interpretable spectral metric in motor neuroscience. Highly appropriate.

Median frequency within band: This captures where within the band the spectral mass is concentrated — the frequency that splits the band power in half. It is less commonly reported but scientifically meaningful. In motor EEG it can reveal, for example, whether MI produces a spectral shift within the beta band (lower beta vs. upper beta dominance) compared to ME. This would be a valuable addition to your analysis.

Additional metric worth considering: the peak frequency within each band (frequency of the PSD maximum). Together with median frequency, these two metrics characterize band shape in complementary ways. This adds minimal computational cost.

One important note on ERD/ERS: Even though your primary metric is PSD, computing the normalized PSD using the baseline (as ERD/ERS percentage change) before extracting AUC and median frequency would significantly improve the ME vs. MI comparison. This is because absolute power levels will vary between sessions, subjects, and electrode impedances. Applying the within-trial baseline normalization first, then computing AUC and median frequency on the normalized spectrum, is methodologically stronger than computing those metrics on raw absolute PSD. This still falls squarely within the PSD framework required by your investigation.

You have five central channels (C3, C1, Cz, C2, C4). 5. Reference Electrode — An Important Consideration
Your reference electrode was placed on the right mastoid, which is ipsilateral to the movement side (right upper limb). This asymmetric reference introduces a systematic bias: recordings from right-hemisphere electrodes (particularly C4) are referenced closer to their source and may appear artificially attenuated relative to left-hemisphere electrodes (C3, C1). For a study explicitly comparing contralateral (C3/C1) and ipsilateral (C4) channels, this is worth acknowledging.

Recommendation: Consider re-referencing to a common average reference or a linked mastoids reference during preprocessing. Common average is standard for high-density EEG (61 channels is sufficient) and will remove the spatial bias introduced by the unilateral mastoid reference. Within-Trial Baseline Normalization: Pros, Cons, and Interaction with Your Rest Reference
What Within-Trial Baseline Normalization Actually Does
For each trial, you divide (or subtract in log/dB domain) the PSD of the active window (t=2–5s) by the PSD of the pre-cue window (t=0–2s) of that same trial. The result expresses power as a change relative to the pre-task state — the classic ERD/ERS framework:


ERD/ERS (%) = [(active_power - baseline_power) / baseline_power] × 100
or in dB:


dB = 10 × log10(active_power / baseline_power)
Pros
1. Removes session-level amplitude differences
This is the most critical benefit for your study. ME and MI were recorded in separate sessions. Absolute EEG power varies between sessions due to electrode impedance, scalp-gel conductivity, subject fatigue, and day-to-day cortical excitability fluctuations. Normalizing to within-trial baseline largely cancels these out, making ME and MI sessions directly comparable on a trial-by-trial basis. Without this, a raw power difference between ME and MI might reflect electrode contact quality rather than neural differences.

2. Removes slow non-stationarities within a session
EEG power drifts slowly over time within a recording session. Within-trial normalization handles this because each trial is self-referenced to its own immediate past.

3. Reduces inter-subject variability
Absolute alpha/beta power varies enormously between subjects (easily a factor of 5–10×). Normalization collapses this variability, making group-level results more interpretable and statistical tests more sensitive.

4. Each trial is independent
The normalization is applied at the trial level before averaging, which is more robust than normalizing the class-average PSD to the class-average baseline.

Cons
1. The baseline is not a neutral resting state
At t=0s the fixation cross already appears, triggering an alerting and attentional response. Alpha suppression associated with visual attention and task readiness begins before the movement cue. This means your "baseline" already contains task-related modulation, and your normalized values technically express change relative to a prepared rather than neutral state. This is a known limitation in virtually all trial-based motor EEG paradigms and must be acknowledged in your methods.

2. It changes your scientific question
Without normalization you ask: "how much power is present during this task?"
With normalization you ask: "how much does power change from pre-cue to during-task?"
These are related but not identical. If your primary interest is in the spectral profile of each movement class as an independent fingerprint (absolute power), then normalization changes the nature of what you are measuring.

3. Rest class baseline may behave differently
This is subtle but important. During rest trials, subjects know (after the cue at t=2s) that no movement is required. The pre-cue window of rest trials (t=0–2s) should be similar to other classes since the cue has not yet appeared — subjects cannot anticipate which class is coming. However, after many sessions, some subjects develop mild anticipatory tendencies. In practice this effect is small but worth acknowledging.

Does It Affect Your "Movement X vs. Rest" Comparison?
The short answer: not substantially, and it may actually improve it.

Here is the key insight. The pre-cue baseline (t=0–2s) should be statistically identical across all 7 classes because subjects have not yet seen the cue — they cannot anticipate whether the upcoming trial will be elbow flexion or rest. This means the baseline power for all classes is drawn from the same neural state.

As a consequence:

If you apply within-trial normalization, rest trials are also normalized to their own pre-cue baseline. You then compare normalized movement class PSD vs. normalized rest class PSD. Since the baselines were equal across classes, the ratio of normalized PSDs is approximately equal to the ratio of absolute PSDs. The relative difference between classes is preserved.

The comparison "how does movement X differ from rest" survives normalization essentially intact, with the added benefit that session-level amplitude confounds are removed.

The practical flow I recommend:


Raw epoch (t=0–5s)
    ↓
Within-trial normalization (active / baseline)
    → produces ERD/ERS values per trial
    ↓
Average across trials per class (including rest)
    → produces class-average normalized PSD
    ↓
Extract AUC and median frequency per band per class
    ↓
Compare movement class X metrics vs. rest class metrics
    AND
Compare ME vs. MI for each class
This way, within-trial normalization and rest-as-reference-class are not competing approaches — they operate at different levels of the pipeline. Normalization cleans up amplitude confounds at the trial level; the rest class serves as your reference condition at the class-comparison level.

One Practical Recommendation
When reporting your metrics (AUC, median frequency) after normalization, they will be in ERD/ERS units (percentage or dB relative to baseline), not absolute power units. A negative AUC in alpha band means net desynchronization relative to baseline — which is exactly the expected finding for motor tasks. Make sure your interpretation and axis labels reflect this. The rest class, having no motor demand, should show minimal change from baseline, making it an excellent and intuitive reference point for the movement classes.

# Answer 4

Recommended Preprocessing Pipeline
Critical Issue with Your Proposed Order
Before detailing each step, your proposed order has a fundamental problem: re-referencing before filtering and ICA is wrong. If large DC offsets, slow drifts, or high-amplitude artifacts are present in any channel, computing the common average reference on contaminated data spreads those artifacts to every channel — including your channels of interest. The correct order is:

Filter → Artifact removal (ICA) → Interpolate bad channels → Re-reference → Epoch → Detrend

Complete Recommended Pipeline
Step 1 — Load Data and Prepare
In MNE, read_raw_gdf() reads GDF natively including event markers. At this stage:

Assign the standard 10-10 electrode montage (g.tec systems use this layout)
Explicitly mark channels 62, 63, 64 as EOG type — MNE uses channel types to guide ICA artifact detection
Note the original reference (right mastoid) for later
Step 2 — DC Offset Removal
Do this before any filtering. g.tec amplifiers with 0.01 Hz analog cutoff can leave non-trivial DC offsets in the digitized signal. Large DC offsets cause Gibbs ringing artifacts at epoch boundaries when you later filter and epoch the data, and they destabilize ICA training.

In MNE: subtract the channel mean across the entire continuous recording per channel. This is a simple, safe operation with no frequency-domain consequences — it removes only the zero-frequency component.

Step 3 — High-Pass Filter at 1 Hz (Continuous Data)
Apply to continuous data, before epoching and before ICA.

Recommended cutoff: 1 Hz, zero-phase FIR (Hamming window, MNE default)

Why 1 Hz and not 0.5 Hz: 0.5 Hz leaves slow physiological drifts (galvanic skin response, slow movement artifacts) that will destabilize ICA. 1 Hz removes these reliably.
Why 1 Hz and not 4 Hz: 4 Hz is unnecessarily aggressive. Your lowest frequency of interest is 8 Hz (alpha), which is 8× the 1 Hz cutoff — more than enough transition band margin. There is no benefit to cutting at 4 Hz.
Why before ICA: This is a hard requirement. ICA decomposes the data based on a covariance matrix estimated from the signal. Slow drifts and DC offsets inflate the covariance structure and produce unstable, non-physiological independent components. The standard recommendation in the field (Winkler et al. 2015, MNE documentation) is a minimum 1 Hz high-pass before ICA.
Why on continuous data: MNE's default FIR filter at 1 Hz has a filter length of approximately 3.3 × sfreq / cutoff = 3.3 × 512 / 1 ≈ 1700 samples (~3.3 seconds). This is longer than your 5-second epochs. Filtering on already-segmented epochs would produce severe edge artifacts. Always filter continuous data first, then epoch.
Step 4 — Bad Channel Detection
Identify bad channels on the filtered continuous data. Two complementary approaches:

Automated detection (recommended as first pass):

pyprep.NoisyChannels (works with MNE): detects channels based on deviation from median amplitude, correlation with neighboring channels, high-frequency noise ratio, and RANSAC (random sample consensus spatial interpolation). This is the most robust automated method available.
Alternatively: mne.preprocessing.find_bad_channels_maxwell() if you have a MaxFilter-compatible system (less relevant for g.tec).
Visual inspection (mandatory second pass):

Always review the PSD plot of all channels to spot channels with elevated broadband noise
Review the raw timeseries for channels with flat lines, excessive amplitude, or sustained artifact bursts
Your interpolation strategy is correct but needs one refinement:

If a channel of interest (C3, C1, Cz, C2, C4) is bad → do not interpolate yet — mark it as bad, complete ICA, then interpolate in Step 7
If any other channel is bad → mark as bad; it will be excluded from ICA and later CAR computation
Important: the number of bad channels directly affects ICA quality. If more than ~10% of channels are bad (>6 channels), ICA results become unreliable. In that case, session-level quality should be flagged.

Step 5 — Independent Component Analysis (ICA)
This is the most critical preprocessing step for your study. Let me be thorough.

Setup:

Method: fastica (default in MNE) or infomax — both are appropriate; infomax is slightly more theoretically motivated for biomedical signals but slower
n_components: set equal to the rank of the data, which is n_good_channels - 1 (because re-referencing to a single reference reduces rank by 1). For your case: approximately 55–58 components depending on bad channels found
Fit ICA on the continuous high-pass filtered (1 Hz) data
Artifact identification — four categories to address:

EOG (blinking and eye movements):

You have dedicated EOG channels (62, 63, 64) — use them
ica.find_bads_eog(raw, ch_name=['eog_ch_name']) in MNE correlates ICA components against EOG channels
Typically identifies 1–3 components; verify topographically (frontal distribution, bilateral)
This is the most reliable automated identification in MNE
ECG (cardiac artifact):

No dedicated ECG channel was mentioned — use ica.find_bads_ecg(raw) which searches for components with QRS-like temporal structure
Cardiac artifact in central channels is less common but can appear; verify with component time course (regular ~1 Hz modulation)
EMG and movement artifacts (ME condition — your critical challenge):

No fully automated method is as reliable as visual inspection here
EMG components have characteristic features: flat or rising power spectrum (broadband, not 1/f), peripheral topography (near the edges of the cap, around temporal/frontal areas where neck and jaw muscles project), and bursty, irregular time course
Movement artifacts (cable movement, electrode pops) appear as: large-amplitude isolated events in the time course, broadband topography
For your ME condition specifically: upper limb muscle EMG has a limited footprint on the scalp (the limbs are physically distant from electrodes), but high-amplitude broadband noise can still project to central areas. Inspect components with significant power in 20–45 Hz range and non-physiological topographies
Recommendation: be conservative — only remove components you are confident are artifactual. Removing genuine neural components is worse than leaving weak EMG residuals when your analysis is band-limited to 8–30 Hz
Residual line noise (50 Hz):

Your analog notch already attenuated 50 Hz. After the digital 45 Hz low-pass (Step 6 below), this is a non-issue. You can skip manual ICA-based line noise removal.
Step 6 — Low-Pass Filter at 45 Hz
Apply after ICA, on continuous data.

Recommended cutoff: 45 Hz, zero-phase FIR

Why 45 Hz and not 40 Hz: Your beta band ends at 30 Hz. A 40 Hz cutoff leaves only 10 Hz of transition margin above beta. A 45 Hz cutoff gives 15 Hz of margin, ensuring zero distortion at 30 Hz. Given that 50 Hz is already notch-filtered analogically, there is no reason to be conservative with the high cutoff.
Why after ICA: ICA was fitted on high-pass filtered data (1 Hz). The low-pass is applied to the ICA-corrected signal. Applying low-pass before ICA would be redundant since ICA doesn't benefit from the high-frequency attenuation.
Combined effect: your final data will be bandpass-filtered 1–45 Hz via two sequential FIR steps, which is mathematically equivalent to a single 1–45 Hz bandpass filter.
Note on the analog pre-filtering: the analog 0.01–200 Hz filter and 50 Hz notch are hardware-applied and cannot be changed. They do not conflict with your digital processing — they simply mean there is no aliased content above 200 Hz and no 50 Hz line artifact remaining. Your digital filtering operates on this already-conditioned signal.

Step 7 — Interpolate Bad Channels
After ICA, before re-referencing.

Using spherical spline interpolation (MNE default: raw.interpolate_bads()). This reconstructs the bad channel's signal from the spatial distribution of surrounding good channels.

Why after ICA: ICA operated without the bad channels (they were excluded). Interpolating now gives you a full-rank channel set before computing the common average reference — if you interpolate before ICA, the interpolated (synthetic) signal would contaminate ICA training.

Why before re-referencing: the common average reference averages across all channels. A missing channel means the average is biased. Interpolating first gives the CAR computation the full spatial complement.

For your channels of interest: if C3, C1, Cz, C2, or C4 were marked bad, they are reconstructed here from neighboring channels.

Step 8 — Re-reference to Common Average
Add the reference electrode back first. In MNE:


raw = mne.add_reference_channels(raw, ref_channels=['Mastoid_R'])
raw.set_eeg_reference(ref_channels='average', projection=False)
Adding the mastoid reference channel back before computing CAR ensures that the average is computed over the true full spatial complement (all 62 EEG channels, including the original reference). Omitting this step biases the CAR computation because the reference channel's signal is implicitly zero — which it is not.

Step 9 — Downsampling to 256 Hz
My firm recommendation: do it. Your apprehension is not warranted here.

After the 45 Hz low-pass filter, the signal contains no frequency content above 45 Hz. The Nyquist frequency for 256 Hz sampling is 128 Hz — far above 45 Hz. There is zero information loss for your analysis (8–30 Hz). The low-pass filter already served as the anti-aliasing filter.

Practical benefits: file sizes halve, all subsequent computations (ICA — already done, epoching, PSD) run on half the data volume. For 14 subjects with two conditions of 420 trials each, this is a meaningful reduction.

In MNE: raw.resample(256) — MNE automatically applies an anti-aliasing filter before resampling.

Step 10 — Epoch Segmentation
Extract epochs aligned to the fixation cross onset (t=0s):


epochs = mne.Epochs(raw, events, event_id, tmin=0.0, tmax=5.0,
                    baseline=None, preload=True, detrend=None)
Set baseline=None at this stage — apply normalization during PSD computation, not here
Keep all 7 event types (6 movements + rest) with their respective event IDs from the GDF markers
preload=True loads all epochs into memory for the subsequent steps
Step 11 — Bad Epoch Rejection
After epoching, inspect for residual artifacts that ICA did not remove (e.g., large-amplitude electrode pops, head movements):

Automated approach — Autoreject (autoreject Python library, Jas et al. 2017):

Learns a rejection threshold per channel per subject using cross-validation
More principled than a fixed global threshold (e.g., ±100 µV)
Produces a rejection log that you can report (percentage of trials rejected per subject)
Manual fallback: if Autoreject is not available, use amplitude-based rejection: peak-to-peak amplitude > 150 µV across EEG channels is a conservative but standard threshold.

Important: track how many trials are rejected per subject and per class. If rejection rates are high (>20% of trials), the subject's data quality should be flagged. Unequal rejection rates across classes could bias your between-class comparisons.

Step 12 — Linear Detrending per Epoch
Apply after epoching, before PSD computation:


epochs.apply_function(scipy.signal.detrend, type='linear')
Or equivalently, mne.Epochs(..., detrend=1) at the epoching step.

With proper 1 Hz high-pass filtering already applied, residual linear trends within a 5-second epoch are minimal. This step is a safety net — it ensures no epoch-level linear slope (due to slow non-stationarities that survived filtering) biases the PSD computation. Specifically important for the within-trial baseline normalization: you want the baseline window (t=0–2s) and active window (t=2–5s) to have comparable low-frequency behavior.

# Answer 5

Assessment of Both Comparison Pipelines
Big Picture: Where They Stand Relative to Your Pipeline
The pipeline we designed in the previous discussion is more thorough and methodologically stronger than either comparison. The most significant omission in both comparison pipelines is ICA — they rely entirely on trial rejection to handle artifacts, which is a cruder and more wasteful approach. With only 60 trials per class, discarding entire trials rather than cleaning them is a meaningful cost. Both pipelines are functional but not optimal for a publication-quality PSD analysis.

First Researchers (Same Dataset) — Analysis
What they did well:

Joint probability and kurtosis as trial-level statistics are principled, EEGLAB-standard metrics. Joint probability flags epochs that are globally improbable given the distribution of the whole recording; kurtosis flags epochs with heavy-tailed distributions (spiky artifacts). Using 5× SD as threshold is standard.
Downsampling to 256 Hz: already in our pipeline.
The two-stage filtering (broad filter first for artifact detection, then narrower filter for analysis) is logical in concept.
What is missing or problematic:

No ICA. All artifact handling is by trial rejection.
Re-referencing comes after artifact removal — they re-reference on already-cleaned data. This is a less principled order, but less damaging here because they had already removed bad channels and marked bad trials. Still, our order (interpolate → re-reference → epoch) is better.
0.3 Hz low cutoff is lower than our recommended 1 Hz. For Butterworth this produces a very gentle slope near DC, leaving slow drifts partially in the data. Not catastrophic for alpha/beta analysis, but suboptimal.
70 Hz high cutoff is unnecessary for alpha/beta analysis and retains EMG content through the artifact-marking phase.
Downsampling before filtering: they downsample to 256 Hz at step 2 before applying the 0.3–70 Hz filter at step 3. This works because MNE's resample() applies its own anti-aliasing filter automatically, and the analog filter already limits the signal to 200 Hz. But explicitly filtering before downsampling is cleaner practice.
No explicit DC removal.
What to incorporate from this pipeline:

Joint probability and kurtosis as supplementary epoch rejection statistics. Autoreject (which we already recommended) is superior because it is data-driven and cross-validated, but if you want to be comparable to prior work on this exact dataset, reporting that you also applied joint probability and kurtosis screening (with 5× SD threshold) gives a direct methodological link to the published work. This is a defensible choice for a paper building on the same dataset.
Colleagues' Pipeline (Different Dataset) — Analysis
What they did well:

The normalized power metric is excellent and directly relevant to your ME condition. This metric is defined as:

normalized_power = power(20–40 Hz) / power(4–40 Hz)
A high ratio means disproportionate high-frequency energy — the signature of EMG contamination. This is exactly the problem you face in the ME condition. It is a targeted, quantitative EMG detector.

Different thresholds for ME and MI is methodologically honest and practically necessary. During ME, genuine muscle activity is expected and some EMG leakage into the EEG signal is unavoidable. Using the same strict threshold for both conditions would reject a disproportionate number of valid ME trials or fail to reject truly contaminated MI trials.

Subject exclusion at >10% trial rejection is a clean, defensible criterion that should be reported in any publication.

What is missing or problematic:

No re-referencing mentioned at all. This is a notable omission. Either it was done and not reported, or they kept the original reference — a significant methodological gap for spectral analysis.
No ICA — same issue as the first pipeline.
0.5 Hz low cutoff: acceptable (lower than our 1 Hz recommendation, but Butterworth with 0.5 Hz leaves more drift than FIR with 1 Hz).
60 Hz high cutoff: broader than needed for alpha/beta; retains some EMG content.
What to incorporate from this pipeline:

The normalized power metric as an explicit EMG trial rejection criterion, with separate thresholds for ME and MI. This is the single most valuable methodological element from either comparison pipeline for your specific study. It adds an EMG-sensitive layer on top of ICA, catching residual contamination that ICA may have missed.
Different amplitude thresholds for ME vs MI — your Autoreject can be run per condition, or you can set manual ME and MI thresholds if needed.
Subject exclusion criterion if >10% of trials are rejected — adopt this as a stated exclusion criterion in your methods.
More or Fewer Steps? The Final Answer
You should have more steps than either comparison pipeline, for the following reason: both pipelines were designed for different primary analyses. The first researchers used this dataset for a different publication — their analysis goals may have tolerated more artifact residual. Your goal is a frequency-domain PSD analysis comparing ME and MI in specific narrow bands (alpha/beta), which is highly sensitive to spectral contamination. A single EMG-contaminated epoch inflates beta power non-linearly.

One step you can simplify: you do not need a separate explicit DC removal step as a distinct pipeline stage. If you apply a 1 Hz high-pass FIR filter as the very first filtering operation on continuous data (Step 3 in our pipeline), the DC component is removed as part of that filter. Remove Step 2 (DC removal) as a standalone step and it is handled within Step 3.

Three Final Notes
1. Steps 10–13 are complementary, not redundant. Amplitude catches large transients; joint probability catches globally abnormal epochs; kurtosis catches spiky artifacts; normalized power catches EMG specifically. Each detects different artifact signatures. Using all four gives you defensible, multi-criteria rejection that is directly comparable to both prior published works on this and related datasets.

2. Maintain a rejection log. For each subject and each condition, record: number of bad channels detected, number of ICA components removed (and type), number of epochs rejected per step and per class. This is essential for the methods section and for detecting systematic data quality issues across subjects.

3. Butterworth vs FIR for your implementation. Both comparison pipelines use Butterworth (via scipy.signal.butter + filtfilt). MNE defaults to FIR. For your analysis, both are acceptable — the zero-phase property is what matters and both achieve it. Since you are working in MNE, stay with the FIR default for consistency with the MNE ecosystem. If you need to justify it, FIR has guaranteed linear phase by construction, while Butterworth achieves zero-phase only through forward-backward application.
